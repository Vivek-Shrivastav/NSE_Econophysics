Chapter 1 — Introduction

What is Econophysics?

The marriage of statistical physics and financial markets — why physicists decided to study stock markets, and what they found that economists had missed.

Imagine you're a physicist who has spent years studying turbulent fluids, critical phenomena, and scaling laws. One day you look at a stock market price chart and say: "Wait — this looks exactly like the patterns I study in physics." That's the birth of econophysics.

The Big Idea

Financial markets are among the most complex systems in the world. Every second, millions of traders react to news, rumours, earnings reports, and each other's behaviour. The result is a time series of prices that looks, at first glance, completely random.

But physicists noticed something: the statistical footprints left by financial markets look remarkably similar to those found in other complex physical systems — turbulent fluids, magnetic systems near phase transitions, and biological networks.

Just as a physicist can't track every molecule in a turbulent fluid but can still predict its large-scale statistical behaviour, an econophysicist can't predict tomorrow's stock price but can characterise the statistical laws governing price fluctuations over time.

Historical Pioneers

Vilfredo Pareto (1897)

Italian economist who discovered that the distribution of wealth follows a power law: y ~ x-ν. He noticed this pattern held across England, Germany, Italy, and Peru. First power-law distribution in social sciences — and the first scale-free observation.

Louis Bachelier (1900)

French mathematician who modelled stock price changes as a random walk in his thesis Théorie de la spéculation. His advisor was Poincaré. He discovered what Einstein would "rediscover" 5 years later — but for prices, not molecules.

Benoît Mandelbrot (1963)

Proposed that cotton price changes follow a Lévy stable distribution — not Gaussian. This "most revolutionary development since Bachelier" implied infinite variance, fractal price paths, and fat tails that standard finance ignored.

Black & Scholes (1973)

Published the option-pricing formula that became the cornerstone of modern finance, assuming geometric Brownian motion (Gaussian returns). This assumption, as we'll see, is an approximation that breaks down in the tails — exactly where it matters most.

Statistical physics has developed powerful tools over the past 50 years — scaling, universality, disordered systems, self-organized criticality. The central insight of econophysics is that these tools apply directly to financial markets, which are among the most complex and data-rich systems in existence.

Why It Matters

The key contribution of physicists to finance is emphasis on empirical data and the language of scaling. Traditional finance often starts with assumptions (returns are normally distributed, markets are perfectly efficient) and builds elegant theories. Econophysics starts with data and asks: what do the statistics actually look like?

When you look carefully, real financial data shows:

Fat Tails

Extreme events (crashes, rallies) happen much more often than a Gaussian model predicts. The 1987 crash was a ~22σ event — impossible under Gaussian assumptions, yet it happened.

Volatility Clustering

Large price changes tend to be followed by large price changes (of either sign). Calm periods cluster together; turbulent periods cluster. This is not random — it has long-range memory.

Scaling Laws

The distribution of price changes looks self-similar across different time scales — a fingerprint of scale-invariant processes, similar to critical phenomena in physics.

Econophysics is empirical, not normative. It describes what markets actually do, not what they should do according to theory. This makes it complementary — not opposed — to traditional finance.
Chapter 2 — Efficient Market Hypothesis

Efficient Markets & Arbitrage

Why stock prices are so hard to predict — and the deep reason for this difficulty isn't lack of information, but an abundance of it.

If a $100 bill were lying on the sidewalk, it wouldn't stay there long. Financial markets work the same way: any predictable pattern gets spotted, exploited, and eliminated. The result? Prices appear unpredictable — not because markets are meaningless, but because they're too efficient at incorporating information.

Arbitrage — The Market's Self-Correcting Force

Arbitrage is the practice of profiting from price discrepancies. It sounds simple, but its systemic effects are profound: arbitrageurs, by exploiting inefficiencies, eliminate those very inefficiencies. Markets correct themselves through the profit motive.

The Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH)

Formulated by Fama (1965) and given mathematical rigour by Samuelson (1965), the EMH states: all available information is instantly reflected in prices.

Samuelson's Martingale Condition
E{ Y(t+1) | Y(0), Y(1), ..., Y(t) } = Y(t)
// Expected tomorrow's price = today's price
// The best forecast of the future is the present
// Past prices carry NO useful information about future prices
Weak Form
Semi-Strong
Strong Form
Reality

Weak Form EMH

All past price information is already reflected in current prices. Technical analysis (using price charts to predict future prices) cannot consistently beat the market. Price changes are unpredictable from the history of price changes alone.

This is well-supported empirically: the autocorrelation of daily returns is essentially zero for most liquid markets.

Semi-Strong Form EMH

All publicly available information (earnings, dividends, economic news) is instantly reflected. Fundamental analysis cannot consistently beat the market. Only private (insider) information could give an edge.

Strong Form EMH

Even private/insider information is already reflected in prices. No one can consistently beat the market. This extreme version is generally considered false — insider trading does exist and can be profitable, which is why it's illegal.

What Actually Happens

In the 1980s, researchers discovered that earnings/price ratios, dividend yields, and term-structure variables could predict long-horizon returns — challenging the strict EMH. The 2013 Nobel Prize went to both Fama (EMH proponent) AND Shiller (behavioural finance critic of EMH) — reflecting genuine scientific uncertainty.

Econophysics perspective: markets are approximately efficient, not perfectly. Residual inefficiencies exist and get exploited. The speed of correction is finite. Statistical footprints of near-inefficiency are measurable.

Algorithmic Complexity — Why Prices Look Random

Consider transmitting π to 125,000 decimal places vs. 125,000 days of Dow Jones data. The number π can be described by a tiny algorithm. The Dow Jones time series cannot be compressed — it contains a huge amount of non-redundant economic information. Its apparent randomness is not emptiness — it's fullness.

The Kolmogorov–Chaitin Insight

A sequence is considered random (unpredictable) if the shortest program that can reproduce it is as long as the sequence itself. Financial time series behave this way — not because they contain no information, but because they contain so much non-redundant economic information that it cannot be compressed.

The efficient market hypothesis is equivalent to saying: the price-time series has maximum algorithmic complexity. You cannot extract a predictive subset because all information is already encoded in the current price.

Chapter 3 — Random Walk

The Random Walk & Central Limit Theorem

The mathematical foundation of modern finance — why stock prices behave like a drunkard's walk, and why that walk converges to a Gaussian in the long run.

Imagine a drunk person standing at a lamppost. Each second, they take a random step left or right with equal probability. Where are they after N steps? They can't be predicted exactly — but we know that their average distance from the start grows as √N. This is the random walk, and it describes stock prices remarkably well.

The Mathematical Setup

Random Walk (Discrete)
S_n = x₁ + x₂ + ... + xₙ
// Where each xᵢ = ±s with equal probability

E{S_n} = 0
// No drift — expected position is zero

E{S_n²} = n·s²
// Variance grows LINEARLY with steps

σ(t) ~ √t
// Standard deviation grows as square root of time
// This is the DIFFUSION law — same as molecules in a fluid!

The Central Limit Theorem

This is perhaps the most important theorem in all of statistics — and it explains why so many things in nature follow a bell curve.

No matter what shape distribution each individual step (xᵢ) follows — uniform, triangular, exponential — as long as the variance is FINITE, the sum of many such steps will always converge to a Gaussian (bell curve). The Gaussian is a universal attractor in the space of probability distributions.
Central Limit Theorem
S̃_n = (x₁+x₂+...+xₙ)/σₙ → PG(S̃) = (1/√2π)·exp(-S̃²/2)
// As n → ∞, the SCALED sum converges to a standard Gaussian
// Requirements: (1) independent xᵢ, (2) FINITE variance
// When these fail → Lévy distributions take over (Chapter 4!)

Speed of Convergence — Berry-Esséen

The Berry-Esséen theorem quantifies how quickly the CLT takes effect. It says the difference between the actual CDF and the Gaussian CDF is bounded by:

|F_n(S) - Φ(S)| ≤ 3ρ / (σ³ · √n)
// ρ = E{|xᵢ|³} — the third absolute moment
// σ = standard deviation
// Convergence speed ∝ 1/√n
// Higher-skew distributions converge MORE SLOWLY

Basin of Attraction

The Gaussian is an attractor in the space of probability distributions. Think of it like a funnel: many different starting distributions all flow toward the Gaussian as n increases. The set of all starting distributions that converge to Gaussian under summing is called the basin of attraction of the Gaussian.

This is analogous to a fixed-point attractor in dynamical systems, or a stable equilibrium in physics. The CLT tells us the Gaussian is the 'ground state' of probability distributions for processes with finite variance.

But what if the variance is infinite? Then the Gaussian is no longer the attractor. Enter Chapter 4...

Chapter 4 — Lévy Stochastic Processes

Lévy Flights & Fat-Tailed Distributions

What happens when the CLT fails? When individual steps can be arbitrarily large? Welcome to the world of Lévy stable distributions — the mathematics of extremes.

Normal (Gaussian) processes are like a calm walk in a park — most steps are small, and extreme steps are astronomically rare. Lévy processes are like a random walk where occasionally you teleport across the continent. In markets, these teleportations are the crashes and bubbles that Gaussian models say should never happen — but do.

Stable Distributions

A distribution is stable if the sum of n independent samples from it has the same functional form (just rescaled). Two examples we already know: Gaussian (α=2) and Lorentzian/Cauchy (α=1).

Lévy and Khintchine solved the general problem: the complete class of stable distributions is characterised by a single parameter α, called the stability index.

Lévy-Stable Characteristic Function (symmetric, zero mean)
ln φ(q) = -γ|q|^α
// α ∈ (0, 2]: stability index
// γ > 0: scale factor
// α = 2 → Gaussian
// α = 1 → Lorentzian (Cauchy)

Asymptotic Tail Behaviour (large |x|)
P_L(|x|) ~ |x|^{-(1+α)}
// Power-law tail!
// E{|x|^n} diverges for n ≥ α when α < 2
// ALL Lévy processes with α < 2 have INFINITE VARIANCE
Infinite variance does NOT mean the distribution is ill-defined — it's perfectly valid mathematically. It means there's no characteristic scale for fluctuations. Just as a power-law distribution of earthquake magnitudes has no "typical" earthquake, a Lévy stable process has no "typical" price jump size.

Scaling and Self-Similarity

Lévy distributions are self-similar: when you scale the x-axis by n1/α, distributions at different time horizons collapse onto each other. This is the fingerprint of a fractal process.

Lévy Scaling (probability of return to origin)
P(S_n = 0) = Γ(1/α) / [πα(γn)^{1/α}]
// For Gaussian (α=2): P(0) ~ 1/√n (standard diffusion)
// For Lévy (α<2): P(0) ~ n^{-1/α} (anomalous diffusion)
// The scaling exponent 1/α is the "fingerprint" of the process

The Power Law Basin of Attraction

Just as the Gaussian is the attractor for finite-variance processes (CLT), Lévy stable distributions are attractors for distributions with power-law tails:

Generalised CLT: If each step has tail P(xᵢ) ~ |x|^{-(1+α)}
Then the sum Sₙ converges to a Lévy stable distribution of index α
// As long as α ∈ (0,2), variance is infinite and Lévy is the attractor
// α=2: transition point — variance becomes finite, Gaussian takes over

The St Petersburg Paradox

This famous 18th-century paradox illustrates why infinite-variance distributions are counterintuitive:

The Game

Flip a coin repeatedly. Win 2n-1 coins if n tails occur before the first head.

n=1: win 1 coin, prob 1/2, expected 0.50
n=2: win 2 coins, prob 1/4, expected 0.50
n=3: win 4 coins, prob 1/8, expected 0.50
...
Total expected win: 0.50 + 0.50 + 0.50 + ... = ∞

How much should you pay to play? The "fair ante" is infinite — yet no rational person would pay even $30. Why? Because this is a scale-free problem. There is no characteristic payout. Power-law distributions are the modern resolution: the distribution of winnings follows a power law, and power-law distributions have no natural scale.

Lévy stable processes were crucial to Mandelbrot's 1963 analysis of cotton prices. He found α ≈ 1.7, meaning infinite variance. This challenged 60 years of financial theory that assumed finite variance. The debate wasn't fully resolved until large-scale empirical studies in the 1990s showed α ≈ 3 — outside the Lévy regime (finite variance), but still with fat tails.
Chapter 5 — Scales in Financial Data

Price Scales & Time Scales

Before analysing financial data, we must choose what to measure and when. These choices have deep consequences for what we find.

In physics, units are sacred — every measurement comes with a unit and uncertainty. In finance, the "units" themselves fluctuate: currencies change value, markets close at night, and the interval between transactions is random. This makes financial data analysis subtler than it first appears.

What Should We Measure?

Price Change Z(t)

Z(t) = Y(t+Δt) - Y(t)

Correct: No nonlinear transformation needed

Limitation: Seriously affected by inflation and scale changes

Log Return S(t) Most Common

S(t) = ln Y(t+Δt) - ln Y(t)

Correct: Naturally accounts for scale changes

Correct: Additive over time

Limitation: Mixes market dynamics with macroeconomic indicators

Return R(t)

R(t) = [Y(t+Δt) - Y(t)] / Y(t)

Correct: Direct percentage gain/loss

Limitation: Sensitive to scale changes for long horizons

Deflated Change Z_D(t)

Z_D(t) = [Y(t+Δt) - Y(t)] · D(t)

Correct: Corrects for inflation

Limitation: No unique choice of deflator D(t)

Key Insight — High Frequency Approximation
For |ΔY|/Y << 1: S(t) ≈ R(t) ≈ C₁·Z(t) ≈ Z_D(t)
// At high frequency (seconds to minutes), all definitions agree!
// For daily data over years, the choice matters significantly
// Most empirical studies use S(t) — the log return

The Problem of Time Scales

Physical Time

Seconds on the clock. Problem: markets close at night and weekends. What happens to prices when the market is closed?

Empirically: weekend variance ≈ 80% of weekday variance. Markets clearly "process" some information while closed.

Trading Time

Only counts hours when market is open. Most common choice in research. But problem: intraday volatility is NOT uniform — it's higher at open and close (the "U-shape").

Transaction Time

Counts individual transactions. Eliminates non-uniform activity but requires tick-by-tick data. Removes one source of randomness (timing), but volume randomness remains.

The intraday U-shaped volatility pattern — high at open, lower at midday, high at close — is analogous to daily patterns in biological systems. It reflects the social organisation of human activity, not purely the information flow.
Chapter 6–7 — Stationarity & Time Correlation

Memory, Correlation, and Volatility Clustering

Does the past predict the future in financial markets? The answer is subtle: prices are nearly uncorrelated, but their volatility has long-range memory stretching back weeks or months.

Flip a coin: heads is +1, tails is -1. Each flip is completely independent of previous flips. Efficient market returns behave like this — uncorrelated. BUT imagine a biased coin whose bias changes slowly over time — sometimes usually heads, sometimes usually tails. The outcomes are still hard to predict, but their variance has memory. This is volatility clustering.

Autocorrelation Function (ACF)

Autocorrelation Function R(τ)
R(τ) = E{ x(t)·x(t+τ) } for zero-mean processes
// R(0) = variance σ²
// R(τ) → 0 as τ → ∞ for stationary processes

Short-Range vs Long-Range Correlation
Short-range: ∫₀^∞ R(τ)dτ is FINITE (exponential decay)
Long-range: R(τ) ~ τ^{η-1} with 0 < η ≤ 1 (power-law decay)
// Long-range: integral DIVERGES — no typical time scale!

The Two Key Empirical Facts

Fact 1: Returns are uncorrelated

The ACF of daily log-returns is essentially zero for lags beyond a few minutes. Price changes are nearly unpredictable from their history.

This supports the Efficient Market Hypothesis. For S&P 500, correlation time ≈ 4 minutes.

Fact 2: |Returns| are long-range correlated

The ACF of |returns| or returns² decays as a power law with exponent γ ≈ 0.3. This means volatility has memory stretching back weeks or months.

This is volatility clustering — "turbulent" periods cluster together, as do calm periods.

Think of the ocean. The direction of each wave crest is unpredictable (like return sign). But the height of waves comes in clusters — storms create many large waves in succession, then calm returns. The wave height (volatility) has memory even when wave direction (return sign) doesn't.

1/f Noise — Long-Range Correlations in Physics

Power Spectrum of Long-Range Correlated Process
S(f) = const / |f|^η with 0 < η < 2
// η=0: white noise (uncorrelated, flat spectrum)
// η=1: 1/f noise (long-range correlated)
// η=2: Wiener process (random walk)
// Volatility of S&P 500: η ≈ 0.7 (1/f^0.7 noise!)
1/f noise appears in transistors, traffic flow, musical rhythms, biological systems, and earthquake sequences. Its appearance in financial volatility suggests that markets share deep structural properties with these complex physical systems. This is not a coincidence — it's the signature of a complex system operating near a critical point.

Stationarity in Markets

Strict stationarity requires that the statistical properties are constant over time. Financial data clearly violates this — volatility is time-varying. At best, we have asymptotic stationarity: as we average over very long periods, the statistics converge.

Chapters 8–9 — Stochastic Models & Scaling

Models of Price Dynamics & Scaling Breakdown

Four competing models for how prices evolve — each capturing different aspects of reality. And the empirical finding that resolves the debate about infinite variance.

The empirical facts we've seen — fat tails, volatility clustering, finite variance — seem contradictory. A fat-tailed distribution usually implies infinite variance (Lévy model). But we observe finite variance empirically. The resolution? The Truncated Lévy Flight — fat tails in the short run that converge to Gaussian in the long run.

The Four Models

1. Lévy Stable
2. Student-t
3. Gaussian Mix
4. TLF

Lévy Stable Model (Mandelbrot 1963)

Idea: Price changes follow a Lévy stable distribution with α < 2 (infinite variance).

Correct: Explains fat tails naturally
Correct: Self-similar (fractal)
Correct: Basin of attraction (stable)

Limitation: Infinite variance — inconsistent with risk measurement
Limitation: Discontinuous price paths
Limitation: Empirically, tails fall off faster than predicted for large |Z|

Student's t-Distribution

P(z) = Cₙ / (1 + z²/n)^{(n+1)/2}

When n=1: Lorentzian. When n→∞: Gaussian. For finite n < ∞: moments of order k are finite only for k < n.

Correct: Can have either finite or infinite variance (tune n)
Correct: Matches empirical distributions at given time horizons

Limitation: Not stable — shape changes with time horizon
Limitation: No scaling relations across time horizons

Mixture of Gaussians (Clark 1973)

Price changes are Gaussian conditional on trading volume. Since volume fluctuates, the unconditional distribution is leptokurtic (fat-tailed).

Correct: Finite variance guaranteed
Correct: Economically motivated (volume drives prices)

Limitation: No scaling relations
Limitation: Shape depends on unknown volume distribution

Truncated Lévy Flight (Mantegna & Stanley 1994)

A Lévy distribution cut off at some maximum jump size ℓ. The truncation preserves Lévy-like behaviour in the centre of the distribution but ensures finite variance.

P_TLF(x) = c·P_L(x) for |x| ≤ ℓ, and 0 otherwise
// c = normalising constant
// Crossover: n× ≈ A·ℓ^α
// For n << n×: Lévy regime (fat tails, non-Gaussian scaling)
// For n >> n×: Gaussian regime (CLT takes over)

Correct: Finite variance (by construction)
Correct: Lévy-like scaling for short time horizons
Correct: Converges to Gaussian for long horizons
Correct: Fits S&P 500 data with α=1.40

Limitation: Artificially sharp cutoff (real version uses smooth exponential cutoff)
Limitation: Assumes i.i.d. increments — misses time-varying volatility

Empirical Findings: S&P 500 Index

Mantegna and Stanley (1995) analysed 6 years of S&P 500 data at 1-minute resolution (493,545 records). They found:

Non-Gaussian Scaling

The probability of return to the origin P(0) scales as a power law: P(0) ~ Δt-0.712

This implies α = 1/0.712 = 1.40 — consistent with Lévy-like behaviour.

But Finite Variance

For |Z| ≥ 6σ, the empirical tails fall BELOW the Lévy prediction. The distribution is truncated — variance is finite.

Scaling holds for 1 to ~10,000 trading minutes. Beyond that, Gaussian takes over.

The Power Law of Rare Events

Gopikrishnan et al. (1999) studied 1,000 large US stocks over 2 years (40 million records). For large returns g = S/σ:

P(|g| > x) ~ x-α with α ≈ 3

The exponent α ≈ 3 is called the "Inverse Cubic Law" — found in virtually every major market worldwide. Since α > 2, variance is finite (consistent with TLF). Since α < 4, kurtosis is infinite. This places real markets in a specific universality class.

Chapter 10 — ARCH and GARCH

ARCH & GARCH: Modelling Volatility Clustering

The workhorse models of financial econometrics — designed precisely to capture the fact that market turbulence comes in clusters, not uniformly.

Imagine the weather. Some days are volatile — lots of thunderstorms. Other days are calm. And importantly, stormy periods tend to be followed by more stormy periods. ARCH/GARCH models capture this same structure in financial markets: past volatility predicts future volatility, even when past returns don't predict future returns.

ARCH(p) — Autoregressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity

Introduced by Engle (1982, Nobel Prize 2003). Key insight: model the conditional variance as a function of past squared returns.

ARCH(p) Model
x_t = σ_t · ε_t where ε_t ~ iid N(0,1)
// x_t: observed return at time t
// ε_t: standardised innovation (white noise)
// σ_t: time-varying volatility

σ_t² = α₀ + α₁x²_{t-1} + α₂x²_{t-2} + ... + αₚx²_{t-p}
// Variance at time t depends on p past squared returns
// α₀ > 0 (baseline variance)
// αᵢ ≥ 0 (each past shock increases current variance)
// Σαᵢ < 1 (stationarity condition)

GARCH(p,q) — Generalised ARCH

Bollerslev (1986) generalised ARCH by also conditioning on past variances, not just past returns squared. This dramatically improves parsimony.

GARCH(p,q) Variance Equation
σ_t² = α₀ + Σᵢαᵢx²_{t-i} + Σⱼβⱼσ²_{t-j}
// αᵢ: ARCH terms — current shock impact
// βⱼ: GARCH terms — persistence of past variance

GARCH(1,1) — The Most Widely Used Model
σ_t² = α₀ + α₁x²_{t-1} + β₁σ²_{t-1}
// α₁ + β₁ = persistence (how long shocks last)
// Typical values: α₁ ≈ 0.09, β₁ ≈ 0.90, persistence ≈ 0.99
// Near-unit persistence → shocks decay very slowly (weeks!)

Statistical Properties of ARCH/GARCH

Fat Tails Emerge Naturally

Even with Gaussian innovations ε_t, the unconditional distribution of x_t under GARCH has fatter tails than Gaussian. The time-varying variance "mixes" Gaussians of different widths, creating a leptokurtic distribution.

The tail index of the unconditional GARCH distribution depends on the persistence α+β.

Volatility Autocorrelation

GARCH(1,1) produces exactly the volatility clustering observed in data: ACF(x_t²) decays as a geometric series with rate β₁.

For empirical data, the ACF of |returns| decays as a power law — slightly slower than GARCH predicts. This suggests fractionally integrated GARCH (FIGARCH) may be more accurate.

GARCH Asymptotic PDF — Tail Behaviour
P(|x| > z) ~ z^{-κ}
// κ determined by: E{(α₁ε²+β₁)^{κ/2}} = 1
// For typical values: κ ≈ 4 to 6
// This is WITHIN the finite-variance regime (κ > 2)
// But heavier tails than Gaussian
GARCH is mathematically equivalent to a specific type of self-similar stochastic process. The connection to physics: the ARCH model can be derived from a multiplicative noise process, similar to models of fragmentation and turbulence cascades. The persistence β₁ ≈ 0.9 is analogous to a physical system operating near (but not at) criticality.
Chapter 11 — Financial Markets and Turbulence

Markets as Turbulent Fluids

One of the most beautiful analogies in all of science: the statistical properties of turbulent fluid velocity fluctuations and financial price fluctuations are strikingly similar.

A turbulent river and a stock market have something profound in common: both exhibit large fluctuations, long-range correlations, and fat-tailed distributions that can't be explained by simple Gaussian models. This isn't metaphor — the mathematical fingerprints are quantitatively similar.

The Kolmogorov Turbulence Framework

In turbulence, energy cascades from large scales to small scales through a hierarchy of eddies. The velocity difference between two nearby points v(x+r) - v(x) has been studied extensively. Key findings:

Kolmogorov Scaling (K41)
〈|v(x+r) - v(x)|^q〉 ~ r^{ζ(q)}
// q: order of moment
// ζ(q): scaling exponents (simple prediction: ζ(q) = q/3)
// Intermittency corrections: ζ(q) is NOT linear in q for q > 3

Financial Analogy: Price Change Moments
〈|S(t, Δt)|^q〉 ~ Δt^{ξ(q)}
// Replace velocity difference with price change
// Replace spatial separation r with time lag Δt
// Multiscaling of moments = multifractality

Turbulence

Velocity PDF: Fat-tailed, non-Gaussian at small scales. Approaches Gaussian at large scales.

Intermittency: Bursts of high activity (eddies) alternating with quiet regions.

Cascade: Energy flows from large to small scales.

Multifractal spectrum: Scaling exponents ζ(q) are non-linear in q.

Financial Markets

Return PDF: Fat-tailed, non-Gaussian at short time scales. Approaches Gaussian at long time scales.

Volatility clustering: Bursts of high volatility alternating with quiet periods.

Cascade: Information flows across time scales? (debated)

Multifractal spectrum: Scaling exponents ξ(q) are non-linear in q.

Quantitative Comparison

Parallel Analysis (Ghashghaie et al. 1996)

A direct comparison of S&P 500 index data and fully-developed turbulence data showed striking visual and quantitative similarity in the PDF shapes across different time/scale lags.

This goes beyond analogy: both systems show non-Gaussian intermittency that evolves as a function of scale in a quantitatively similar way.

However, the mechanisms are different. In turbulence, there's a physical energy cascade from large to small eddies. In markets, the "cascade" (if it exists) is likely information-based — news at long time scales propagating to short time scales. The analogy is statistical, not mechanistic.

This connection is one of the most striking results in econophysics. It suggests that markets, turbulence, and perhaps many other complex systems share a common mathematical structure — possibly related to self-organized criticality or multiplicative noise. Understanding the mechanism could unlock insights in both physics and finance.
Chapters 12–13 — Correlation & Portfolio Taxonomy

Correlations Between Stocks: Networks and Trees

Stocks don't move independently. Understanding how they correlate — and how to organise them into a meaningful hierarchy — is the foundation of portfolio management.

When the market crashes, almost all stocks fall together. But some are more correlated with each other than with the rest. Oil stocks move together. Tech stocks move together. Understanding this structure helps investors diversify — and helps physicists understand the emergent organisation of the market.

The Correlation Coefficient

Pearson Correlation Coefficient
ρᵢⱼ = [〈SᵢSⱼ〉 - 〈Sᵢ〉〈Sⱼ〉] / [σᵢσⱼ]
// Sᵢ = log return of stock i
// σᵢ = standard deviation of stock i's returns
// ρᵢⱼ ∈ [-1, +1]
// ρ=1: perfect co-movement. ρ=-1: perfect anti-movement. ρ=0: independent

Correlation Matrix Properties

For N stocks, we have an N×N symmetric correlation matrix. Random matrix theory (RMT) predicts that if all stocks were independent, the eigenvalues of the correlation matrix would follow the Marchenko-Pastur distribution.

In real markets, most eigenvalues follow the RMT prediction (noise), but a few — corresponding to market-wide and sector-wide correlations — are significantly larger. These carry the true economic information.

From Correlation to Distance

Correlation Distance Metric
dᵢⱼ = √(2(1 - ρᵢⱼ))
// dᵢⱼ ∈ [0, √2]
// Perfectly correlated (ρ=1): d=0 (same stock!)
// Uncorrelated (ρ=0): d=√2
// Anti-correlated (ρ=-1): d=2 (maximum distance)
// This satisfies metric axioms: d(i,j) > 0, d(i,j)=d(j,i), d(i,k) ≤ d(i,j)+d(j,k)

Minimum Spanning Tree — Taxonomy

Using the distance matrix, we can construct a Minimum Spanning Tree (MST) — the simplest network connecting all N stocks with no loops, using the shortest possible total distance. The MST extracts the most important pairwise correlations.

Think of it like connecting N cities with roads using the minimum total road length. The MST selects only the most "important" connections — the ones that carry the most correlation information. In practice, the MST groups stocks by sector (IT companies cluster together, energy companies cluster together) — revealing the underlying economic taxonomy of the market.

Ultrametric Space

The MST defines an ultrametric space — a special type of metric space where the triangle inequality is strengthened to:

d(i,k) ≤ max[d(i,j), d(j,k)]
// Ultra-triangle inequality (stricter than ordinary triangle inequality)
// Ultrametric = hierarchical structure
// Natural representation: a dendrogram (tree diagram)
Ultrametric spaces appear in spin glass physics — the study of disordered magnetic materials. The "taxonomy" of stocks discovered through correlation analysis is mathematically equivalent to the hierarchy of metastable states in a spin glass. This deep connection suggests that financial markets and disordered physical systems belong to the same mathematical universality class.
Chapters 14–15 — Derivative Securities

Options, Black-Scholes & Real Markets

How do you price the right to buy a stock in the future? The Black-Scholes formula is elegant, Nobel Prize-winning — and built on assumptions that econophysics reveals to be approximations.

Imagine you want to protect yourself against Apple stock falling. You buy an "insurance policy" — the right to sell Apple at today's price even if it falls. How much should this insurance cost? Black and Scholes (1973) answered this question with a beautiful formula that earned them the Nobel Prize — and launched the multi-trillion dollar derivatives industry.

Types of Derivative Contracts

Forward Contract

Obligation to buy/sell an asset at a specified price on a future date. Both parties are locked in. Price today is based on today's price plus cost of carry.

Futures

Like a forward, but standardised, traded on exchanges, and with daily mark-to-market settlement. Reduces counterparty risk. Used for commodities, currencies, indices.

Options

Call: RIGHT (not obligation) to BUY at price K.
Put: RIGHT to SELL at price K.
Premium = price of this right.

Speculating and Hedging

Speculation (Example)

Stock price S₀ = $100. Buy a call option with K=$105 for $5.

If price rises to $120: profit = $120 - $105 - $5 = $10 on a $5 investment (100% return!)

If price stays at $100: lose entire $5 premium (100% loss)

Options provide leveraged exposure — amplified upside AND downside.

Hedging (Risk Insurance)

Hold 1,000 shares worth $100,000. Buy 1,000 put options with K=$95 for $2 each = $2,000 total.

If price falls to $70: options pay $25,000. Net loss = $30,000 - $25,000 + $2,000 = $7,000 (not $30,000!)

Options provide downside protection — like insurance for your portfolio.

The Black-Scholes Formula

Black-Scholes Call Option Price
C = S₀·N(d₁) - K·e^{-rT}·N(d₂)
// C: call option price (fair value)
// S₀: current stock price
// K: strike price (the price you have the right to buy at)
// r: risk-free interest rate
// T: time to expiry
// σ: volatility of the stock
// N(·): cumulative normal distribution

d₁ = [ln(S₀/K) + (r + σ²/2)T] / (σ√T)
d₂ = d₁ - σ√T
// Key insight: C depends on σ but NOT on the expected return!
// This is the magic of risk-neutral pricing (delta hedging)

Where Black-Scholes Breaks Down

Black-Scholes assumes that log-returns are Gaussian (geometric Brownian motion). As we've seen, this is false: returns have fat tails, and volatility is not constant. This creates the "volatility smile" — implied volatility varies with strike price, which it should not under the BS model.

Historical vs Implied Volatility

Historical: measured from past returns. Implied: backed out from observed option prices using BS formula.

They should be equal in the BS world. In practice, implied volatility varies with strike (the "smile") and is often higher than historical volatility — especially for out-of-the-money puts (investors pay a premium for crash protection).

Discontinuous Returns

Black-Scholes assumes continuous price paths. Real prices jump discontinuously — stocks can open 20% below yesterday's close following bad news. These jumps cannot be hedged with delta-hedging, breaking the BS risk-neutrality argument.

Jump-diffusion models, stochastic volatility models, and Lévy process models are used to correct for this.

History of Ideas

Historical Timeline

100 years of econophysics, from Bachelier's thesis to modern big-data empirical analysis.

1897
Pareto's Power Law
Vilfredo Pareto discovers that wealth distribution follows a power law y ~ x⁻¹·⁵. First empirical power law in social sciences.
1900
Bachelier's Thesis
Louis Bachelier (advised by Poincaré) models stock prices as a random walk in Théorie de la spéculation. Anticipates Einstein's 1905 work on Brownian motion by 5 years.
1905
Einstein's Brownian Motion
Einstein derives the diffusion equation for particle motion, inadvertently rediscovering Bachelier's random walk. Establishes the mathematical foundation for stochastic processes in physics.
1953
Kendall's Empirical Work
Maurice Kendall finds that stock prices are nearly uncorrelated from day to day — initially viewed as a puzzle, later interpreted as evidence for market efficiency.
1963
Mandelbrot's Lévy Hypothesis
Mandelbrot proposes that cotton price changes follow a Lévy stable distribution with α≈1.7. "The most revolutionary development since Bachelier." Introduces power laws and fat tails to financial theory.
1965
Samuelson & Fama: EMH
Samuelson proves mathematically that properly anticipated prices fluctuate randomly. Fama formulates the Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH) and tests it empirically.
1973
Black-Scholes Formula
Fischer Black and Myron Scholes publish the options pricing formula, assuming geometric Brownian motion. Launches the modern derivatives industry. Later earns the Nobel Prize (1997).
1982
Engle's ARCH Model
Robert Engle introduces ARCH — the first model to explicitly capture time-varying volatility and volatility clustering. Nobel Prize in Economics 2003.
1986
Bollerslev's GARCH
Tim Bollerslev generalises ARCH to GARCH — a more parsimonious model that became the workhorse of financial econometrics.
1990s
Econophysics Emerges
Stanley, Mantegna, and collaborators begin systematic empirical studies of financial markets using physics methods. The term "econophysics" is coined. New journals and conferences are launched.
1994
Truncated Lévy Flight
Mantegna and Stanley propose the TLF — reconciling Mandelbrot's fat tails with the empirical observation of finite variance in S&P 500 data.
1999
Inverse Cubic Law
Gopikrishnan et al. analyse 40 million stock price records and find the universal power-law exponent α≈3 for the tail of the return distribution — the same in US, UK, Japan, and Germany.
2000s
Network Science & Markets
Mantegna applies minimum spanning trees to model portfolio taxonomy. Correlation matrices analysed using random matrix theory. Financial networks studied using complex network methods.
